Summary

This documentation presents a beginners guide on how to use Altium and its operations. After reading through this guide, you should have a basic understanding of the important concepts that are used in building PCBs. Please note that this is to help a new user get started understanding and reinforcing the most practical applications of Altium Designer.

New Project

After getting Altium installed on your machine, you can go ahead and start your new project. Open up the application to get started.

  • Select "File"
  • Choose "New"
  • Click "Project"

You should be presented with a following screen:


Figure 1: Creating a new Altium project

The "default" option will always be selected as the starting point. When you do make your selection, the new project will be added to your screen. New documents are assigned with the ".PrjPcb" extension. Next you will need to add either an existing schematic or create a new one. For simplicity, start with creating a new schematic.

  • Right-click on the new .PcbPrj file that you recently created.
  • Highlight "Add New to Project".
  • Select "Schematic".

On your "Projects" panel, you should see a new document get added to the project. The default name for your schematic document is "Sheet1.SchDoc"


Figure 2: Creation of a new Schematic document

On the main screen, you should also see a grid representing how to plot your design.


Figure 3: Blank Schematic page

You will also want to create a new Pcb document. The steps are just the same as mentioned above for creating a schematic except for one detail. Instead of selecting "Schematic" pick the "PCB". On the "Projects" panel should be a new document added with the pcb extension. The default name will be "PCB1.PcbDoc".


Figure 4: PCB and Schematic document addition

Note: The "Source Documents" means that the documents added will be located in the directory. Make sure anything you add for your project is located in the "Source Documents" or a new folder will be made called "Free Documents" that are not directly linked to your project.

The new Pcb document you added should also be displayed on the main screen. What should appear is a black rectangle.


Figure 5: Pcb board screen

Panels

The "Panels" section is used for viewing different entries that coincide with your project. To locate the "Panels" menu, look at the bottom-right corner of your Altium designer.


Figure 6: location of the Panels button

When you click on the "Panels" button, it will display a list of options related to your project like so:


Figure 7: Panel display. Note that areas with a check mark are panels you currently have open on your project.

Most of the time, besides constructing your boards on the 'Projects" panel, you will be editing setting in your "Properties" box depending on components you add. When you click on a panel section to edit, the box will pop up in the middle of your screen.


Figure 8: Properties panel on first pop-up.

You can pin the screen you select over on either the left-hand or right-hand side of your screen by clicking on the panel and dragging it to the desired location.


Figure 9: Properties panel snapped into the right-hand side of Altium.

If you click the thumbtack icon located in the middle of Figure 9, you can pin the the properties tab on the side when closing the screen.

Making a Schematic

When you begin making a schematic, you can start with the general components that come with Altium Designer installed. If you have another database you have access to, or other libraries stored on a local drive, you may use those as well. To begin creating a schematic, you can look at the toolbar that appears above the schematic page:


Figure 12: Schematic toolbar

To start a schematic

  • Click on the "Panels" button
  • Select the option "Components"

You will get a new tab that will pin to the right-hand side of your screen called "Components". The dropdown menu will have two selections of integrated libraries: Miscellaneous Devices and Miscellaneous Connectors.


Figure 13: Prebuilt integrated libraries

When you click on one of .IntLib choices, you should see a list of already populated components. For example, this is a screen you would see if you chose "Miscellaneous Devices".


Figure 14: Default components selection regarding devices.

Note: If you are wanting to find more parts or looking for a specific component, you can choose "Manufacturer Part Search".

Now you can select the component you are looking for and can drag the device over to your schematic. You can also see what the device looks like in the preview and in 3D in the "Models" pane located in "Components"


Figure 15: Schematic placement and preview

Note: An important piece to remember is regarding net labels and port connections. Net label connections only exist on the schematic sheet they are on, not in-between multiple schematic sheets. Ports provide connections in between schematic sheets and no on the schematic sheet they are on.

Compile Design

When you finish designing your schematics you will want to compile. Ideally, everything will compile with zero errors being issued. But with designing anything in preliminary stages, you will often find errors and warnings when you first compile the design.

To compile

  • Right-click the project file
  • Select "Compile PCB Project"

Figure 16 Compile project

Once you compile your project, you will then need to check your messages for warnings and errors. If your message box doesn't appear, click on "Panels" and select "Messages".


Figure 17: Error List

Depending on your messages, there can be some quick fixes

For floating pin errors

  • Right-click on the error/warning in the "Messages" panel containing the errors.
  • Select "place specific no ERC error violation...".

This will let you place a cross on the pin not being used and will clear the warning/error message.

For no driving source errors

This happens to pins that are not being put in use and have not been completely eliminated.

  • Go to "Place" on the top screen.
  • Select "Directives".
  • Choose "No ERC"

Figure 18: No ERC selection

This will allow you to place an x over any pins that do not need to be used.

Electrical Rules/Footprints

You will need to check your basic electrical rules you want your project to follow. To find the electrical rules

  • Right-click "Project"
  • Select "Project Options"

Here you will find multiple tabs regarding various rules for your current project.


Figure 19: Project options list

Rules here can be modified to suit your project. If you want to switch back, just press the 'Set to Default Installations" below. The last thing you need to check are the footprints that can be found in the database. This is more precautionary than necessary since validation should succeed.

To check footprints

  • Go to "Tools"
  • Select "Footprint Manager"

You will be presented with a component list that is included in the design. To validate the list

  • Hold the shift key and select that final entry.
  • On the "View and Edit Footprints" screen, select all the components.
  • Press "Validate".

Figure 20: Validating footprints

Once validated, we know the components have been found and can be used on the PCB.

PCB Transfer

After completing your schematic sheets, you can now transfer your on to designing your PCB.

To transfer your schematics to the PCB

  • Select your schematics sheet.
  • Choose "Design" from the top menu.
  • Click "Update PCB".

You will then be presented with the following screen called the "Engineering Change Order".


Figure 21: Engineering change order
  • Click "Execute Changes"
  • check for any errors by selecting the checkbox "Only Show Errors"

If no errors exist, switch over to your PCB and you should find new "rooms" depending on your schematic sheets included.

PCB Rules

PCB settings also have there own set of rules that must be adhered to when moving on to the PCB design. Before you begin, its recommended to set your rules prior before building the circuit board as you see fit. Designing before modifying or correcting the rule set can lead down to further complications on your project.

To View the PCB rules

  • Select your PCB document
  • Click "Design" at the top menu
  • Choose "Rules...".

This will open you up to a new screen called the "PCB Rules and Constraints Editor". Here you can enforce or disable rules as you see fit.


Figure 22: Rules and Constraints window
Note: Have full acknowledgement of what rules you disable. Rules that are not in effect can cause massive fatal errors when designing your pcb board.

There are two types of rules that exist in PCB construction:

  • Unary
  • Binary

Unary rule will only focus on one object. In contrast, a binary rule will focus on two objects. If you need to add a new rule that isn't covered by the default option, you can do the following.

  • Under "Design Rules" right-click the selection underneath (i.e Routing).
  • Select "New Rule".

Note: When you make a new rule, whatever option selected on the right-hand side will appear to duplicate with a "_1" on the end and increment with each new rule established.

Figure 23: New rule creation

Rules come into action based on the queries made for the selected object.


Figure 24: Object queries page

You can set the scope for the query selection and keeping the constraints mode in check. For example, if you had a high voltage net you want to use a net class option instead of using "All - All" If you change the scope, you may also need to change the Priorities section as well. Click the "Priorities" button below to see the values.


Figure 25: Edit rule priorities panel

In all cases, if you have a new scope, you want the scope to be above "All - All" and "All - All" should be in the lowest priority. This is because Altium uses a method that whatever the high rule catches, the lower rules will not see. For example, in "All - All" scenario, any special rules may not get applied.

PCB Component Placement

At this point, you can start moving components onto your PCB board and get started making your creations. On your PCB, you can start moving boards or rooms around where you desired.

Note: If you try to move a component outside of its room, you will start compiling errors.

When you select components to move, you have two types of object selections.

  • Right-to-Left
  • Left-to-Right

If you are creating a rectangle moving right-to-left, it will select every component it touches. This is true if the wire connected to a component outside the rectangle is connected. As long as the wire is caught in the rectangle, that attached component will be highlighted. Using the left-to-right option will only select everything that rectangle contains. What this means is if you only highlight part of a component, it won't get picked up in the selection. You can also see your designs in 3d by pressing "3" and can see your work in 3d by holding shift key. To move back into 2d, press number "2".


Figure 26: 3D mode

PCB Routing

When routing your PCB board, you have options on which layers to use during routing. On the bottom of your PCB board screen, you will see all the layers that are associated with the PCB.


Figure 27: PCB layer association

To route manually or Interactive Routing

  • Be on your PCB.
  • Click "Design".
  • Select "Interactive Routing" or do ctrl+w.

To route on multiple layers

  • Click on area to begin.
  • Move to desired location and left-click to create a vertex or vertices.
  • Press "+" to create a link in the next layer.

At this point you will want to start doing a polygon pour. A polygon pour can help cover idle space on a board. The idle space covered can help connecting to ground wires and improve power efficiency.

For polygon pours

  • Select "Place".
  • Choose any of the polygon pour designs that works for you.
  • Right-click to exit the polygon pour mode when you complete.

Design Rule Check

When nearing the end of your PCB layout you will want to run a design rule check to validate your layout. When running a check on your layout, you can generate a more detailed list of any errors that may still be in your board. To start:

  • Click "Tools".
  • Select "Design Rule Check".
  • Choose "Run Design Rule Check".

This will generate a new report called a "Design Rule Verification Report". The report will show rule violations and a more in-depth report about the errors. You messages panel will also display the errors as well.


Figure 28: Verification report

While still on your PCB screen, there will be a new selection in the panels called "PCB Rules and Violations". Opening the scree, you can see the errors and highlight what you need to fix. Double clicking the error messages will take you to the screen and allow you to fix the mistake.

Fabrication

The last step before moving into production is to generate the fabrication files.

  • Go to "File".
  • Select "Fabrication Outputs".
  • Click "Gerber files".

Note: Gerber files is considered the industry standard. Unless noted otherwise, you fabrication output will usually be using Gerber files.

You will get a screen called the gerber Setup that will go over the layers and how you want your drilling to proceed.


Figure 29: Gerber setup screen

General: Determines how our units will be plotted and the how we want the output format to be filed.

Layers: What layers you want to be plotted. The default will be absent.

Drill Drawing: How you want to set up your drill pairs.

Apertures: Used for uploading or creating aperture files.

Advanced:: More specific settings regarding other changes.

You will do the same to generate the drill files only this time you will select "NC drill files". The other option you will select for the end is the pick and place files.

  • Go to "File".
  • Highlight "Assembly Outputs".
  • Choose "Generates pick and place files"

All three of these options will generate new files in the "Generated" folders for Altium Designer.

Board Review

Before sending out your project for fabrication, you want to go through an extensive board review. This is to help catch any errors as well as make any improvements on the current design.

Schematic Document Review

  • Are all pins hooked up correctly on all IC's.
  • All grounds are routed.
  • If a net label GND is being used is it tied to a ground iuobject somewhere.
  • Cloned resistors and capacitors have correct values.
  • Cloned resistors and capacitors have correct Digikey part numbers.
  • Cloned resistors and capacitors have correct footprints.
  • IC's have correct footprints for part ordered.
  • All parts are annotated.
  • All components used are in a project included library.
  • Compile Project.

PCB Document Review

  • Import Changes from PCB Project.
  • Apply Design Rules (OSH Park) for hole size.
  • Apply Design Rules (OSH Park) for Minimum Annular Ring.
  • Apply Design Rules (OSH Park) for trace width.
  • Layer Stackup all layers visible.
  • No island polygons on PCB.
  • Ground pins of components aren't floating
  • Polygons with high voltage differences have adequate distance from each other.
  • USB lines are placed with differential routing tool.
  • All strings are 30mil text height.
  • All traces are 10mil w/ exception of differential usb lines.
  • Are via stitching groups down and polygons poured around them.
  • Ensure all polygons aren't shelved.
  • Design Rule Checks (Zero Errors).
  • No net antennas.
  • Double check silk screen to solder mask.
  • No unrouted nets.
  • Are decoupling capacitors and power cap arrays located as close to pins as possible?
